## The problem with how we measure labor markets

Most labour market information systems are built to report on the past. They tell you what jobs existed last quarter, what wages were paid last year, which sectors contracted a decade ago. They are archives dressed up as dashboards.

The people who need labor market information most — a young graduate deciding whether to enroll in a vocational program, a ministry official designing a reskilling intervention, an employer trying to understand where talent will come from in three years — need something different. They need information that helps them make decisions under uncertainty, not information that confirms what already happened.

## What a decision-useful LMIS actually requires

A decision-useful LMIS needs to answer three questions that current systems almost never address:

**What will be demanded, not just what was.** This means integrating real-time job posting data, employer surveys, and sectoral investment pipelines — not just administrative employment records that lag by 18 months.

**What does "skill" actually mean to employers.** Most systems track occupational codes and credential levels. But employers hire for adjacencies — the mechanical engineer who can read a sensor dashboard, the accountant who can build a basic model. Skills taxonomy work that treats competencies as atomic and transferable is more useful than credential pyramids.

**Where the friction is, not just where the gap is.** A labor market with a documented skills gap is not the same as a labor market with a solvable problem. Geographic mismatch, credential non-recognition, discrimination, and capital constraints all look like "skills gaps" in aggregate data. A good LMIS tells you which one you're actually dealing with.

## What this means in practice

Building this kind of system is not primarily a data problem. The data exists or can be collected. It is a political economy problem — who owns the system, who has incentives to keep it current, who gets to act on its findings.

The most durable LMIS implementations I've seen are the ones that were designed from the beginning around a specific decision a specific institution needed to make. Not "we need a labor market observatory" — but "the Ministry of Education needs to decide which TVET programs to expand in the next budget cycle, and here is the data architecture that makes that decision possible."

Everything else follows from that.
